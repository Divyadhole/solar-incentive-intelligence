# Key Findings — US Solar Incentive Intelligence

## The Rate Question Settles the Debate

People assume solar economics are driven by sun. They're not — at least not first.

The primary driver of commercial solar returns is the electricity rate you're displacing.
Connecticut has 43% more sunlight than the UK but 23¢/kWh utility rates.
Nevada has excellent sun and 12¢/kWh rates.

Connecticut IRR: **19.4%**. Nevada IRR: **13.3%**.

The sun matters. The rate matters more.

---

## Arizona Is a Solid Market — Not Because of Sun Alone

Arizona capacity factor: 20.1% (top 5 nationally, tied with Nevada).
Arizona commercial rate: 11.7¢/kWh (moderate — not high, not low).
Arizona incentives: 25% state tax credit + full retail net metering.

That combination puts Arizona at rank #5 nationally with a 13.0% IRR
and 7.2-year payback. For a company like SOLON operating in the Southwest,
the Tucson and Phoenix markets are consistently bankable.

---

## The Worst Market: Washington State

Washington has solid sun (capacity factor 14.9%) and a large commercial sector.
But its average commercial electricity rate is **7.6¢/kWh** — the cheapest in the country.

Washington IRR: 6.1%. Payback: 14.8 years.

When power is that cheap, solar almost never pencils for a commercial building
without exceptional incentives or specific time-of-use tariff structures.

---

## What State Incentives Actually Do to the Numbers

States with "Strong" incentive programs (per DSIRE):
- Average IRR: 16.4%
- Average payback: 6.1 years

States with no state-level program:
- Average IRR: 9.8%
- Average payback: 10.2 years

That is a **4.1-year payback difference** purely from state incentive programs.
New Mexico (10% state tax credit + NEM) vs neighboring Texas (no state credit):
New Mexico 13.0% IRR vs Texas 9.7% IRR on the exact same system.

---

## Net Metering Policy Is the Hidden Variable

Full retail net metering (NEM 1.0 / NEM 2.0 in most states) means every kWh
you export is worth the same as every kWh you import.

California just switched to NEM 3.0 which dramatically cut export compensation.
This dropped California commercial solar economics despite still having
the second-highest electricity rate in the lower 48.

Hawaii has no net metering at all (self-supply only) — yet has the best solar
economics because the grid rate is so high that self-consumption alone justifies the project.

---

## Data Notes

EIA rates: 2023 annual averages, commercial sector. Rates vary significantly
by utility within a state — a building on Arizona Public Service (APS) vs
Tucson Electric Power (TEP) can face different rates. This analysis uses
state averages as a first-pass filter.

NREL capacity factors: annual average. Actual project performance varies by
roof pitch, azimuth, shading, and specific location. Use PVWatts
(pvwatts.nrel.gov) for site-specific modeling.

DSIRE data: current as of January 2024. Incentive programs change frequently.
Always verify current program status at dsireusa.org before quoting.
